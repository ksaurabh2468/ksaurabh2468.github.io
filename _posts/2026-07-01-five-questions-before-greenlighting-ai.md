The best AI project I ever ran is one you'll never hear about, because we killed it in week
two. The demo was exciting, the team was keen, and the honest answer to a few plain questions
was *no*. Walking away freed the budget for something that shipped and mattered.

Program leadership on AI work is, more than anything, the discipline of asking uncomfortable
questions early — while they're cheap. These are the five I run every initiative through
before I'll commit real money to it.

## 1. What decision does this change?

If I can't name a specific decision — one a person or a system makes — that this project will
improve, I stop. "Better insight" is not a decision. "The line lead diverts a flagged unit to
manual review instead of shipping it" is. AI that doesn't change an action is a science
project wearing a business case.

> Start from the decision and work backwards to the model. Never the reverse.

## 2. Who owns the decision today — and do they want help?

A model that feeds a decision nobody owns dies on contact with the org chart. Worse is a
decision someone owns *and guards*. If the person whose judgment we're augmenting sees the
system as a threat rather than a tool, no accuracy number will save the rollout. Adoption is
decided in that human relationship, long before deployment.

## 3. What does being wrong cost?

Every model is wrong sometimes. The question is whether that's survivable. I want the cost of
a false positive and a false negative in *concrete* terms — a wasted unit, a missed defect, a
mis-routed customer — before I care about the accuracy target. The tolerable error rate is a
business decision derived from that cost, not a technical aspiration set in advance.

## 4. Do we have the data, honestly?

Not "is there data somewhere" — every company believes it's data-rich until you ask for a
labeled, representative, accessible sample. I want to see it in week one. The gap between the
data people *think* they have and the data a model can actually learn from is where most
timelines quietly go to die.

## 5. What's the boring version?

Before committing to AI, I ask what a rules engine, a better dashboard, or a well-placed SQL
query would get us. Sometimes the answer is "80% of the value for 10% of the cost and none of
the model risk." A good program leader is happy to talk their own team *out* of the exciting
solution when the boring one wins. The goal is the outcome, not the machine learning.

## The pattern underneath

Notice that four of the five questions aren't about AI at all. They're about decisions,
ownership, cost, and data. That's not an accident — it's the whole point. The model is the
easy part. Whether the project *should exist* is the hard part, and it's the part a program
leader is uniquely positioned to get right.

Ask these five early. The best outcome is sometimes a fast, cheap *no* — and that no is worth
more than most yeses.
