A while back I co-authored a peer-reviewed paper — *"Computer Vision Enabled for Corrective
Packaging,"* published by World Scientific Publishing, Singapore — while the underlying system
was live and shipping. Doing research and delivery at the same time is uncomfortable in ways I
didn't expect, and clarifying in ways I didn't either. A few notes.

## Writing it down exposes what you don't understand

You can ship a system that works without fully understanding *why* it works. Delivery rewards
results; it doesn't audit your explanations. Writing for peer review is the opposite — every
claim has to survive a skeptical stranger who wasn't in the room.

The act of formalizing what we'd built surfaced assumptions we'd been carrying implicitly for
months. Some held up. A couple didn't, and finding that on paper was far cheaper than finding
it on the line.

> If you can't explain why your system works to someone who wasn't there, you don't yet know
> why it works. You just know that it does.

## Product time and research time run at different speeds

Delivery is measured in sprints; research is measured in the patience of reviewers. Holding
both is a genuine tension — the paper is never the thing on fire, so it's always the thing
that slips.

What worked for me was treating the writing as a slow background process with a
non-negotiable minimum: a little, consistently, protected from the delivery schedule rather
than squeezed into its gaps. Waiting for a free week never produced one.

## Rigor and shipping aren't opposites

The lazy story is that researchers are rigorous and builders are fast, and you pick one. The
work taught me they feed each other. The discipline of research — define your terms, measure
honestly, state your limitations — makes for *better* production systems, not slower ones.
And the constraints of production — real data, real failure costs, real users — make for
research that's grounded instead of theoretical.

The paper made me a more careful engineer. The engineering made the paper honest.

## Why bother, on top of a delivery schedule?

Because the discipline compounds. Publishing forced a standard of clarity and evidence on my
own thinking that I've carried into every program since. I don't reason about a system quite
the same way once I've had to defend it to reviewers who owe me nothing.

You don't need a publication to get this. But you do need the *habit* it builds: writing your
work down, plainly, for someone who will push back. It's one of the highest-leverage things
I've done for the quality of my own thinking — and it costs a notebook and the willingness to
be wrong on the page.
